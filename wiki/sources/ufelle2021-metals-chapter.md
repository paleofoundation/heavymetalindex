# Ufelle & Barchowsky 2021 — Toxic Effects of Metals (Casarett Essentials 4e, Ch 23)

## TL;DR

This is Chapter 23 "Toxic Effects of Metals" from Casarett & Doull's Essentials of Toxicology, Fourth Edition (McGraw Hill, 2021), authored by Alexander C. Ufelle and Aaron Barchowsky. The chapter is the abbreviated "Essentials" companion to the full 9th-edition parent textbook, and operates as a summary rather than a primary-literature synthesis. Its cadmium subsection runs approximately PDF pages 770 to 775 of the 2021 Essentials PDF and covers Exposure, Toxicokinetics, Toxicity (with subheadings for nephrotoxicity, chronic pulmonary disease, skeletal effects, cardiovascular effects, neurotoxicity, and reproductive health effects), Carcinogenicity, and Treatment. The chapter's value to the wiki is primarily corroborative: it confirms, in a 2021 textbook of record, that the field consensus established in the Handbook on the Toxicology of Metals (Nordberg, Nogawa, Nordberg 2015) and the regulatory documents has not shifted materially. It contributes two practice-clinical notes and one framing signal absent from the other ingested sources: the explicit statement that no effective clinical treatment exists for cadmium intoxication and that chelation therapy produces significant adverse effects; a notably less-hedged cardiovascular framing ("strong association" with peripheral vascular disease, with cadmium possibly mediating a fraction of the negative effect of smoking on peripheral artery disease); and the observation that pregnant women appear to accumulate more cadmium than nonpregnant women.

## Methods (brief)

The chapter is a textbook-summary treatment drawing on peer-reviewed primary literature and on the parent 9th edition of Casarett & Doull's Toxicology: The Basic Science of Poisons. It is not a primary research article and does not synthesize new quantitative risk assessments. Its utility for the wiki is as a 2021 snapshot of what two US-based toxicology educators (Ufelle at Pittsburgh, Barchowsky at Pittsburgh) presented as the current state of knowledge for graduate-level and professional training. The chapter does not cite its individual primary sources inline in the "Essentials" format; provenance traces back to the parent Casarett & Doull's 9th edition and to the underlying primary literature, which the wiki's other sources (Handbook Ch 32, ATSDR 2012, EFSA 2009) cite directly.

Limitations: the chapter is intentionally compact and does not reach the level of detail present in the Handbook on the Toxicology of Metals chapter 32 on toxicokinetic model structure, on itai-itai disease epidemiology, or on the dose-response meta-analytic underpinnings of regulatory reference values. It also does not capture the newer (2019 to 2022) occurrence data on cadmium in cocoa and chocolate that the JECFA 91st meeting ingest brings to the wiki.

## Implications

- Certification: the "no effective treatment, chelation produces significant adverse effects" clinical-practice statement is durable and important for HMT&C defensibility. The absence of an effective clinical intervention reinforces the argument that dietary-cadmium reduction through certification is the appropriate population-level response rather than treatment after the fact. Consumer-audience HMT&C and wiki content on cadmium should reflect this: the prevention-over-treatment framing is medically grounded, not a marketing assertion.
- Courses: the Essentials is the teaching companion most educators will reach for first; its coverage is a reasonable starting point for a module on cadmium, with the Handbook chapter 32 and the ATSDR profile providing the depth for subsequent lectures.
- App: the chelation-ineffective note is material for consumer-facing app content. Where users receive exposure estimates that approach or exceed reference values, the app should communicate that dietary exposure reduction is the only practical mitigation, not a clinical intervention. Reproductive-audience content (pregnant and potentially-pregnant users) should reflect the chapter's observation that pregnant women accumulate cadmium more than nonpregnant women.
- Microbiome: not addressed in this chapter.

Two minor variances from the Nordberg 2015 Handbook chapter that the wiki captures transparently rather than averaging:

First, the chapter reports inhalation absorption of cadmium at 10 to 60 percent; the Handbook chapter reports 10 to 50 percent. The lower bound agrees; the upper bound differs slightly. The wiki cites Nordberg 2015 as the primary source for inhalation absorption and notes this chapter's slightly higher upper bound rather than averaging the two or privileging one without explanation.

Second, the chapter's cardiovascular framing is notably less hedged than the Handbook chapter's. Ufelle and Barchowsky describe a "strong association" between cadmium and peripheral vascular disease and raise the possibility that cadmium partially mediates the negative cardiovascular effect of smoking. The Handbook chapter, written six years earlier, treats cardiovascular effects as "variable" and does not reach the same level of claim. Both positions are recorded on the wiki's [[metals/cadmium]] page without resolution; the synthesis page flags the framing shift as an open question for the eventual synthesis pass. Neither position is strengthened or softened to reconcile the two; honest acknowledgment of the gap is the drift-protection posture required by the CLAUDE.md firewall rule.
