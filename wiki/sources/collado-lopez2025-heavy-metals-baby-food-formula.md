# Collado-Lopez et al. 2025 — Heavy metals in baby foods and infant formulas

## TL;DR

This scoping review synthesized global evidence from 75 studies published between 2014 and 2024 covering 580 baby foods and 251 infant formula items. The review grouped baby foods into cereals, fish/fish mixes, fruits and vegetables, meat/meat mixes, mixed foods, rice/rice mixes, and roots/tubers; it grouped infant formulas by stage, specialty need, and primary protein source. Lead, cadmium, and arsenic were detected in more than 60% of both baby-food and formula items, while mercury was less frequently studied and detected.

## Key numbers

- Baby foods: heavy metals were detected in 65% of evaluated determinations; Pb was detected in 69%, Cd in 72%, As in 73%, and Hg in 34% of baby-food items evaluated.
- Infant formulas: heavy metals were detected in 63% of evaluated formula determinations; Pb was detected in 74%, Cd in 61%, As in 63%, and Hg in 42% of infant-formula items evaluated.
- Baby-food categories with notable medians or exceedances included fish/fish mixes for Pb, As, and Hg; rice/rice mixes for Pb and As; cereals for Cd; and roots/tubers for Pb detections.
- For rice/rice-mix baby foods, the review reported median Pb of 0.008 mg/kg and median As of 0.048 mg/kg; 31% of detected rice/rice-mix items exceeded the Pb maximum level used by the authors and 30% exceeded the As maximum level.
- For cereals, the review reported median Cd of 0.013 mg/kg, with 17% of detected cereal items exceeding the Cd maximum level used by the authors.
- For fish/fish-mix baby foods, the review reported median As of 0.165 mg/kg and median Hg of 0.016 mg/kg; 89% of detected fish/fish-mix items exceeded the As maximum level used by the authors.
- For infant formulas, the review reported that stage 1 and stage 2 formulas had the highest median Pb concentrations by stage grouping, both at 0.015 mg/kg.
- By primary protein source, Pb was detected in 84% of soy-based formula items and 73% of cow-based formula items; Cd was detected in 91% of soy-based formula items and 44% of cow-based formula items.
- Fifteen of the included health-risk-assessment studies identified risks from intake of these products; the authors highlighted rice products for infants aged 6 months and older and stage 1/2 formulas for infants 12 months and younger.

## Methods (brief)

The authors followed Joanna Briggs Institute and PRISMA-ScR methods. Eligible sources included peer-reviewed studies, national reports, and nonprofit reports that measured Pb, Cd, As, and/or Hg in baby foods or infant formulas, reported sample size and measured concentrations, and stated analytical methods. The review converted reported average concentrations to mg/kg and compared them with international maximum levels selected by the authors.

## Limitations

The paper is a scoping review, not a single harmonized sampling dataset. The authors report substantial heterogeneity in category definitions, analytical reporting, geography, formula-stage metadata, and product-description specificity. Several HMTc Category 1 rows are narrower than the review's groupings, so this source supports broad row scaffolding but not precise powder-vs-ready-to-feed, puree-specific, or brand-level claims.

## Implications

- Certification: Useful as an A-tier global evidence map for Category 1 prioritization, but not sufficient for setting row-specific numerical thresholds by itself.
- Courses: Strong overview source for why infant and young-child products require category-specific contaminant monitoring.
- App: Supports initial app-risk flags for rice/rice mixes, cereals, fish/fish mixes, roots/tubers, soy formula, and cow-based formula pending ingredient-level profiles.
- Microbiome: No direct microbiome endpoint.
