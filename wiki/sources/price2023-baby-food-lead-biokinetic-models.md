# Price et al. 2023 — Lead biokinetic models and baby-food exposure

## TL;DR

This paper compares regulatory lead biokinetic models and uses FDA's 2023 proposed baby-food lead action levels as a case study. It is most useful for Category 1 as a lead-specific risk-modeling source, not as a product concentration survey. The authors conclude that soil and dust explain most modeled variance in infant blood lead levels under their U.S. assumptions, while food and water together contribute less than 15% of variance.

## Key numbers

- FDA's 2023 proposed lead action levels discussed in the paper are 10 ppb for fruits and vegetables and 20 ppb for root vegetables and dry infant cereals.
- FDA estimated that these action levels would reduce dietary lead intake at the 90th exposure percentile from 2.02 micrograms/day to 1.5 micrograms/day for the sum of fruits, root vegetables, and dry infant cereal.
- In the authors' model, a 30% dietary lead reduction scenario produced an approximately 8% reduction in the percentage of children aged 1-2 years and 1-5 years with blood lead levels exceeding 3.5 micrograms/dL.
- The authors reported that soil explained more than 50% and dust about 35% of modeled variance in ingestion and blood lead levels, while water and food during the first and second years explained less than 15% combined.

## Methods (brief)

The authors compared IEUBK, ICRP, and AALM lead biokinetic models, then used IEUBK in a 10,000-iteration probabilistic exposure assessment with distributions for soil, dust, water, and food intake. The analysis focused on modeled central-tendency blood lead levels rather than measured product concentrations.

## Limitations

The study is lead-only and includes a declared conflict-of-interest statement: the authors are or were employed by Stantec ChemRisk, a consulting firm that advises government, corporate, legal, and scientific clients, including some food and beverage companies. The paper should be used for modeling and source-contribution context, not as the sole source for claims about infant-food safety.

## Implications

- Certification: Useful for separating product lead limits from total child lead-exposure management.
- Courses: Useful to explain why food lead policy matters but does not exhaust lead-risk mitigation.
- App: Supports lead-specific uncertainty language; food-risk estimates should not be framed as total blood-lead prediction.
- Microbiome: No direct microbiome endpoint.
