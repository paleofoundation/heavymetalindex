This page is a longer-form orientation for readers arriving at the Heavy Metal Index for the first time. The [landing page](/) is briefer.

## What the Heavy Metal Index covers

The index organizes the published literature on heavy metals in food around a consistent taxonomy. Eight metals are in scope: lead, cadmium, inorganic arsenic, total arsenic, methylmercury, total mercury, nickel, aluminum, chromium, and tin. (The distinction between inorganic and total arsenic, and between methylmercury and total mercury, is maintained throughout the wiki because the toxicological and regulatory implications differ sharply by species.)

Pages are organized into eight principal sections. Each [metal](/metals) has a page describing toxicology, typical exposure routes, food sources, regulatory limits, testing methods, microbiome effects where documented, and vulnerable populations. Each [ingredient](/ingredients) of interest, such as rice, cocoa, leafy greens, spinach, or carrots, has a page describing why the commodity accumulates specific metals, typical concentration ranges by source and variety, processing effects, and mitigation options. Each [product category](/products) describes the heavy-metal risk profile of consumer-facing food categories, including infant foods, chocolate, tea, seafood, and protein powders. Each [regulation](/regulations) describes a specific threshold, action level, or guidance from a specific jurisdiction (United States Food and Drug Administration, European Food Safety Authority, Codex Alimentarius, California Proposition 65, and others). Each [testing method](/testing) describes the analytical principle, limits of detection, typical applications, and pitfalls of a laboratory approach (inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry, atomic absorption spectrometry, X-ray fluorescence, speciation protocols, sampling plans). Each [health](/health) page addresses toxicology, exposure routes, dose-response, and vulnerable populations. Each [microbiome](/microbiome) page addresses the subset of microbiome biology for which metal exposure is the primary mechanistic link; broader microbiome medicine is covered at [WikiBiome](https://wikibiome.com). Each [source](/sources) has its own page summarizing what the source measured, what it found, and which wiki pages cite it.

## What the Heavy Metal Index does not cover

Clinical metallomics in medicine, such as copper metabolism in Wilson's disease or nickel hypersensitivity in endometriosis, is out of scope and covered at WikiBiome. Occupational exposure in industrial settings is out of scope; the occupational-hygiene literature is a distinct field with its own canonical references. Heavy metals in environmental media outside the food system (ambient air, drinking water contamination not via food preparation) are addressed only where the literature links them back to dietary exposure or supply-chain provenance. Brand-specific product rankings, test results for named branded products, and certification program records are not published here. The separate [Heavy Metal Tested and Certified](https://heavymetaltested.com) program hosts its own publications.

## How to read a wiki page

Every page carries frontmatter metadata at the top of the source file (not always visible on the rendered page, but accessible in the repository). Every numeric claim is sourced; follow the citation to see the method, sample size, and provenance. Every regulatory value is preserved at the exact value from the agency source, without rounding, and the original units are retained. Where a value would otherwise require conversion to be comparable across sources, the conversion is shown rather than concealed.

The wiki distinguishes between claims the literature firmly supports, claims the literature tentatively supports, and open questions where sources disagree or evidence is thin. Uncertainty is stated explicitly rather than smoothed over. The goal is calibrated honesty about what the literature actually supports.

## Relationship to certification

The Heavy Metal Index reports what the literature supports. The Heavy Metal Tested and Certified program, operated by the same Paleo Foundation but at a different domain and with different editorial policy, sets certification thresholds that may be tighter than the literature floor on purpose (as a market-ratcheting instrument), looser than the literature floor for feasibility reasons, or aligned with external regulatory frameworks. The [methodology page](/methodology) and the [about page](/about) describe the editorial independence of this wiki from the certification program; the design rationale is summarized in the Foundation's published statement on the separation.
