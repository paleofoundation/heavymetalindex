# Source Inclusion Protocol

This page defines how documents enter the Heavy Metal Index corpus, how they are classified once found, and what kinds of material are excluded, deferred, or treated as secondary context. It is the public answer to the question: what gets into the Index, on what basis, and with what limits?

## Principle

The Index is designed to be broad at corpus level and selective at claim level.

The corpus boundary is intentionally generous: peer-reviewed literature, government and intergovernmental regulatory material, major toxicology references, and source-backed context documents that bear on heavy metals in food, food ingredients, supply chains, remediation, testing, or exposure interpretation.

Public claims, however, are narrow. A source may belong in the corpus but still fail to support a specific public number, product-row table cell, or regulatory comparison. Inclusion in the corpus is not the same thing as approval for public synthesis.

## Source classes

The corpus accepts five major source classes.

1. Peer-reviewed occurrence, toxicology, analytical-method, exposure, or regulatory-interpretation literature.
2. Government and intergovernmental documents, including FDA, EFSA, EPA, ATSDR, CDC, JECFA, Codex, OEHHA, and related bodies.
3. Textbooks, reference monographs, and other licensed secondary authorities used for synthesis or method context.
4. Reputable non-governmental and trade-publication material used as attributed context when it adds category visibility, market signal, or enforcement context.
5. Query or briefing outputs written inside the wiki, when they are traceable to existing source pages and serve as durable synthesis artifacts.

## Inclusion criteria

A source is eligible for ingest when all of the following are true.

- The document is relevant to one or more target analytes, matrices, product categories, ingredients, regulations, testing methods, supply-chain pathways, or exposure interpretations within the Index scope.
- The document has stable enough provenance to be cited: source title, authorship or agency attribution, date or edition, and a retrievable or privately held source file.
- The document can support at least one of the following: occurrence evidence, regulatory evidence, toxicology context, testing-method interpretation, exposure interpretation, source-family provenance, or dispute/correction context.
- The document can be routed into the persistent wiki structure, even if only as context-only evidence or a logged data gap.

## Exclusion and deferral rules

The following material is excluded from public ingest or is deferred to non-public systems.

- Brand-specific certificates of analysis, internal lab reports, and client-confidential testing records do not enter the public Index.
- Marketing copy, press releases, unsourced blog posts, and general news coverage do not support public numeric claims. They may serve as leads for primary-source retrieval but are not themselves treated as public evidence.
- Documents outside the Index scope, such as purely occupational, clinical, or environmental papers with no meaningful food or supply-chain connection, are excluded or routed to sister properties where appropriate.
- Files whose provenance cannot be verified, whose contents do not match their filename or claimed identity, or whose source text cannot be trusted are deferred until clarified.

## Duplicate, update, and source-family handling

Duplicate handling is source-family aware.

- Exact duplicates are not ingested twice.
- Near-duplicates are collapsed to the best-supported version, with the relationship documented.
- Reissued guidance, corrigenda, and updated agency documents are linked as source families rather than silently replacing the earlier record.
- Where a short agency web summary and a full PDF guidance describe the same operative document, the Index may preserve both as a source family if that is necessary for provenance, current-status verification, or legal posture.

## Retractions and corrections

Retracted or materially corrected literature is not silently erased.

- If a source is retracted, the source page remains as a historical record and is visibly marked as retracted or superseded.
- If a journal or agency issues a correction, the correction is linked, the affected claim classes are reviewed, and any public pages that used the original document are updated.
- If the original source remains useful only as historical or provenance context after correction, its Evidence Fitness and public-use posture are downgraded accordingly.

## Approval boundary

A source can sit in the corpus in one of three broad public-use states.

- present in corpus only;
- ingested into the evidence layer but not yet approved for public numeric use;
- approved for public use on specific claim classes.

This is why a product page may legitimately show routed sources that still need extraction, row-fit review, or promotion. The correct public behavior is to preserve visible incompleteness rather than to pretend the source is absent or fully resolved.

## What this means in practice

The Index is not claiming that every file in the raw collection is equally strong, equally relevant, or equally ready for public use. It is claiming something more defensible: that the corpus is deliberately assembled, provenance-tracked, and routed through a visible review structure before public conclusions are drawn from it.
