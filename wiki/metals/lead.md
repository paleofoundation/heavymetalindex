# Lead

_This page draws on the ATSDR 2020 Toxicological Profile for Lead ([[sources/atsdr-lead-toxprofile-2020]]), the EFSA CONTAM 2010 Scientific Opinion on Lead in Food ([[sources/efsa-lead-contam-2010]]), the JECFA 72nd meeting 2010 re-evaluation that withdrew the PTWI ([[sources/jecfa-72nd-lead-2010]]), the EPA IRIS chemical assessment for inorganic lead ([[sources/epa-iris-lead-2004]]), the CDC Blood Lead Reference Value documentation ([[sources/cdc-blood-lead-reference-value]]), the OEHHA Proposition 65 lead listing ([[sources/oehha-lead-prop65-listing]]), the FDA Closer to Zero Pb baby food guidance ([[sources/fda-ctz-Pb-babyfood-2025]]), and three biochemistry-mechanism reviews ([[sources/tamagno-freeman-2025-pb-glutamate]], [[sources/ordemann-austin-2016-pb-zinc-finger]], [[sources/huang-2020-pb-alad-threshold]])._

## Overview
<!-- audience: regulator, educator, consumer -->

Lead is a non-essential heavy metal and a pervasive environmental contaminant. It enters the human food system through atmospheric deposition from historical leaded gasoline use, soil contamination from leaded paint and industrial emissions, contact with legacy lead-containing food processing equipment and water distribution materials, and uptake by crops from contaminated soil. The metal is absorbed at higher rates by children than by adults (5 to 15 percent in adults, with children retaining more of what they absorb), distributes to soft tissues in the short term and to bone over decades, and is excreted slowly through urine and faeces. Blood lead has a half-life of approximately 30 days; bone lead has a half-life of 10 to 30 years and serves as an internal reservoir that releases lead during pregnancy, lactation, and bone resorption.

The defining feature of lead's regulatory framing is the absence of a demonstrated effect threshold for the most sensitive endpoint, developmental neurotoxicity in young children. JECFA withdrew its previous provisional tolerable weekly intake of 25 µg/kg b.w./week in 2010, EFSA concluded the same year that no PTWI is health-protective, EPA IRIS does not derive an oral reference dose, and ATSDR does not derive Minimal Risk Levels for lead. Operational US public-health action runs through CDC's blood lead reference value of 3.5 µg/dL and FDA's Closer to Zero Interim Reference Levels (2.2 µg/day for children, 8.8 µg/day for women of childbearing age, derived from the BLRV with a 10x safety factor). California Proposition 65 lists lead under both cancer and reproductive-toxicity categories with separate quantitative implementation thresholds.

## At a glance
<!-- audience: consumer -->

Three facts that matter most for a consumer trying to interpret lead exposure.

First, no major regulatory body has identified a safe blood lead level for children. Lead's developmental neurotoxicity occurs at the lowest measurable blood concentrations, and effects on cognition, behavior, and brain development have been documented at blood lead levels well below the population threshold the CDC uses for screening (3.5 µg/dL). The framing for lead is "minimize exposure as far as practicable," not "stay below a specific number."

Second, children are at materially higher risk than adults. Children absorb a larger fraction of ingested lead, retain more of what they absorb, and are exposed during developmental windows when the brain is most susceptible to lead's effects. Pregnancy is also a high-risk period because bone lead accumulated over a lifetime can mobilize during pregnancy and lactation, transferring to the fetus or nursing infant. The FDA Closer to Zero program targets these populations specifically.

Third, the population-level dietary contributors to lead exposure are cereals (the largest contributor in the EFSA 2010 European analysis), vegetables, and root vegetables for children, with dust and soil as important non-dietary sources especially for young children playing on the ground or in older homes. Cocoa products and certain spices also carry elevated lead concentrations relative to staple foods. Lead-soldered cans, lead-glazed ceramics, traditional or imported cosmetics, and water from lead service lines remain meaningful exposure pathways in some settings.

## Toxicology
<!-- audience: regulator, educator -->

Lead is primarily a developmental neurotoxicant. The 2010 EFSA opinion identified developmental neurotoxicity in young children as the most sensitive critical endpoint, with a Benchmark Dose Lower Confidence Limit (BMDL01) of 12 µg/L blood lead, corresponding to a dietary intake of 0.50 µg/kg body weight per day. Cognitive deficits, behavioral abnormalities, and reduced IQ have been documented in cohort studies at blood lead levels below the BMDL, supporting the regulatory position that no safe level exists. The molecular mechanism of lead neurotoxicity operates through multiple pathways: substitution for divalent cations (Ca²⁺ and Zn²⁺) in zinc-finger transcription factors essential for brain development (Ordemann and Austin 2016), disruption of glutamatergic signaling through interference with NMDA, AMPA, and metabotropic glutamate receptors and the EAAT, VGLUT, and SNAT transporter families (Tamagno and Freeman 2025), induction of oxidative stress and excitotoxicity, and inhibition of DNA repair.

Cardiovascular effects are the second canonical adult endpoint. EFSA 2010 derived a BMDL01 of 36 µg/L blood lead for systolic blood pressure effects (1.50 µg/kg b.w./day dietary equivalent). Renal effects are the third, with a BMDL10 of 15 µg/L for chronic kidney disease prevalence (0.63 µg/kg b.w./day dietary equivalent). Additional documented endpoints include immunological effects, hematological effects (lead inhibits δ-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase, ALAD, in heme biosynthesis; Huang et al. 2020 detected the dose-response threshold using Generalized Additive Models in occupationally exposed workers), and reproductive effects (the basis of California Prop 65 reproductive listing).

Lead is classified by EPA as B2 (probable human carcinogen, animal evidence with inadequate human evidence) and by California Prop 65 as a chemical known to cause cancer; quantitative cancer dose-response is not derived in the IRIS file.

## Typical exposure routes
<!-- audience: regulator, educator -->

Dietary intake is one of several meaningful routes for the general population. Adult gastrointestinal absorption of ingested lead is approximately 5 to 15 percent, with adults retaining typically less than 5 percent of an ingested dose; children absorb a substantially higher fraction and retain more of what they absorb. Inhalation of airborne lead is operationally minor since the phase-out of leaded gasoline but remains relevant in occupational settings, in proximity to active smelting or battery manufacture, and from re-entrainment of historically deposited soil and dust. Dust and soil ingestion is the dominant non-dietary route for young children (hand-to-mouth behavior, contaminated indoor dust from deteriorating leaded paint).

Bone lead accumulated over a lifetime is itself an internal exposure source: bone lead mobilizes during pregnancy, lactation, postmenopausal bone resorption, and any other period of accelerated bone turnover, providing endogenous lead exposure to the bloodstream and to the fetus or nursing infant.

## Food sources
<!-- audience: regulator, educator, app -->

EFSA 2010 identified cereal products as the dominant population-level dietary contributor to lead exposure across European Member States. Vegetables (root vegetables in particular for children) contribute substantially. Specific high-concentration matrices include cocoa products and chocolate, certain spices (turmeric, chili powders subject to documented adulteration episodes), lead-glazed traditional ceramics in contact with food, and food contained in legacy lead-soldered cans (now rare in most jurisdictions but remaining in some imported goods).

Adult dietary lead exposure across European populations ranges from 0.36 to 1.24 µg/kg b.w./day average, up to 2.43 in high consumers. Child dietary lead exposure ranges from 0.80 to 3.10 µg/kg b.w./day average, up to 5.51 in high consumers. Infant exposure ranges from 0.21 to 0.94 µg/kg b.w./day. The EFSA developmental-neurotoxicity BMDL01 of 0.50 µg/kg b.w./day is therefore exceeded by mean adult European exposure on the upper end of the country-specific range, by mean European child exposure across most of the range, and by high-consumer European child exposure by approximately 10-fold.

## Testing
<!-- audience: educator -->

Blood lead concentration is the standard exposure biomarker, measured by graphite furnace atomic absorption spectrometry, ICP-MS, or LeadCare anodic stripping voltammetry (point-of-care). The CDC reference value of 3.5 µg/dL is denominated in whole blood. Bone lead measurement by K-shell X-ray fluorescence (KXRF) is used in research settings to assess cumulative lead body burden. ALAD activity in red blood cells (Huang et al. 2020) is a sensitive biochemical biomarker of lead's interference with heme biosynthesis. Urinary lead reflects recent excretion and is used in occupational monitoring but is less informative than blood lead for general-population exposure assessment because of its short integration window and influence by recent intake.

## Open questions
<!-- audience: regulator, educator -->

The dominant open question for lead is operational rather than scientific. Every major regulatory body now agrees that no safe blood lead level has been demonstrated for children, but this consensus has not produced a single international reference value: JECFA withdrew its PTWI without replacement, EFSA reports BMDLs without setting a TWI, EPA IRIS contains qualitative discussion only, and ATSDR does not derive MRLs. Operational US action runs through CDC BLRV → FDA IRL → CTZ matrix-specific action levels, but this chain is not internationally harmonized. HMT&C calibration to "lead exposure as far below typical as practicable" is therefore methodologically defensible at the framing level but does not have a single numeric anchor.
