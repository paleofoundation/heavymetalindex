Ingredient suppliers are a distinct user class in Heavy Metal Index. Their public questions are not identical to brand, retailer, or regulator questions, even when they point to the same evidence base.

## What suppliers need from the public Index

For ingredient suppliers, the public Index should support at least the following functions:

- identify the metals and species of concern for an ingredient;
- identify higher-risk derivative forms and concentration steps;
- identify the public regulatory references that may apply;
- explain what a usable certificate of analysis should contain;
- explain what comparability limits matter before a buyer, brand, retailer, or auditor uses a result.

## Supplier-facing sections that belong on ingredient pages

Ingredient pages should grow beyond contamination narrative alone. A supplier-ready ingredient page should support these public sections.

### Supplier snapshot

The fast summary of:

- ingredient name and relevant aliases;
- metals and species of concern;
- higher-risk and lower-risk derivative forms;
- basis issues that commonly create bad comparisons;
- major downstream product categories affected.

### Testing requirements

The analytes, species, basis, and units a supplier should test, together with the public comparability notes that matter for downstream use.

### COA and evidence packet requirements

A buyer-usable evidence packet should identify at least:

- analyte and species;
- result value and units;
- basis (`as sold`, `dry weight`, `as consumed`, `prepared for feeding`, or other clearly stated basis);
- laboratory and method;
- LOD and LOQ;
- lot or batch identity;
- sample date and sample definition;
- origin and, where relevant, region or harvest context.

### Incoming spec framework

The public page should separate:

- regulatory ceilings or action levels;
- public evidence benchmarks and distribution context;
- notes that stricter internal specifications may exist outside the public site.

### Sampling and lot acceptance

Public guidance should explain what sampling questions matter: lot definition, composite versus grab sampling, unit count, retest triggers, and requalification cadence.

### Origin, region, and derivative risk

Suppliers need public context on geology, soil, irrigation, cultivar or species differences, and processing steps that concentrate or redistribute metals.

### Change-control triggers

The page should identify changes that may require renewed testing or buyer notification, including supplier change, origin change, harvest change, processing change, equipment change, packaging change, or ingredient derivative change.

### Downstream product impact

Ingredient results do not affect every finished product equally. Public pages should explain how concentration, dilution, extraction, milling, defatting, drying, blending, or fractionation change downstream risk.

### Audit readiness

The page should help a supplier anticipate what a brand, retailer, auditor, or regulator will expect to see in a defensible records package.

### Evidence gaps and limits

The page should say openly where the public evidence is still too weak to support percentile math, supplier ranking, or a defensible public benchmark.

## Public versus private supplier questions

### Appropriate for public Ask the Index

- Which metals and species should a rice ingredient supplier test?
- What fields should a cocoa powder COA include?
- Why is one derivative form riskier than another?
- What public regulatory values apply to this ingredient?
- What public evidence gaps remain before a stronger benchmark can be defended?

### Private Analytical Workbench only

- Does this supplier lot support release?
- Which supplier is the outlier in our portfolio?
- How does supplier A compare with supplier B over six months?
- Does this COA support our internal spec?
- What reduction is needed for this supplier to hit our internal percentile target?

Those questions depend on confidential datasets and belong in [[analytical-workbench]], not in the public assistant.
